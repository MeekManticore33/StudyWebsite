Chapter 1

Setting the Stage

Key Vocabulary & Concepts

Mental Model
A person's internal representation of how the world works — a set of learned rules, beliefs, or ways of thinking shaped by background, education, culture, and experience. Information systems are defined by the mental models of the people who design them.
Source: Course slides, Ch 1; see also Senge, P., The Fifth Discipline (1990).
Example: MS Excel treats 0 as FALSE and nonzero as TRUE — reflecting the mental model of engineers who designed early programming languages in the 20th century.
Curse of Knowledge
A cognitive bias where once a person knows something, they find it difficult to imagine not knowing it, leading to poor communication. When experts cannot remember what it was like to be a beginner, they overestimate what others understand.
Source: Heath, C. & Heath, D., Made to Stick: Why Some Ideas Survive and Others Die (2007).
Example: A professor with 25+ years of Excel experience may explain homework worse than a TA because they can no longer imagine what it's like not to know Excel.
5-Component Information System (IS) Model
An information system consists of five interrelated components: Hardware, Software, Data, Processes, and People. Hardware and Software form the computer side; Processes and People form the human side; Data serves as the bridge. Automation moves work from the human side to the computer side.
Source: Kroenke, D., Experiencing MIS, 2e, p. 30; Course slides, Ch 1.
Example: An online ordering system includes servers (hardware), the website code (software), customer records (data), the order fulfillment workflow (process), and the employees and customers who use it (people).
Gartner Hype Cycle
A model developed by Gartner that describes five phases new technologies typically pass through: (1) Emerging Innovation, (2) Peak of Inflated Expectations, (3) Trough of Disillusionment, (4) Slope of Enlightenment, and (5) Plateau of Productivity.
Source: Gartner, Inc., gartner.com/hype-cycle; Course slides, Ch 1.
Example: Generative AI may currently be near the Peak of Inflated Expectations, with massive investment but corporate adoption rates among large firms peaking at only ~14%.
Investment ≠ Adoption ≠ Diffusion
High levels of investment in a technology do not guarantee that organizations or consumers will actually adopt or widely use it. When speculative (anticipatory) demand exceeds actual demand, a bubble forms. Market hype occurs when speculation outpaces real usage.
Source: Apollo Global Management (2025), U.S. Census Bureau BTOS; Course slides, Ch 1.
Crossing the Chasm (Geoffrey Moore)
A framework expanding on the Hype Cycle, explaining that technology adoption responds to three types of demand: (1) actual demand (customers who will buy and use), (2) perceived market readiness (what firms believe the market wants), and (3) anticipatory/speculative adoption (early adopters betting on future value). The "chasm" is the gap between early adopters and the mainstream market.
Source: Moore, G., Crossing the Chasm (1991); Course slides, Ch 1.
Self-Directed Learning
The ability to find trustworthy and accurate sources applicable to a current problem and distinguish between fact and opinion. Most problems have already been solved — the skill is in locating credible solutions.
Source: Course slides, Ch 1.

Chapter 1 Quiz

Question 1
Marcus is building a scheduling app for his student organization. He designs the interface so that meeting times are always shown in 24-hour military format (e.g., 14:00 instead of 2:00 PM). When other members complain they find it confusing, Marcus is genuinely surprised — he grew up in a military family and has always read time this way.
Which concept best explains why Marcus designed the interface this way?
  • A. The Gartner Hype Cycle caused Marcus to adopt unproven technology too early
  • B. Marcus's mental model of how time should be displayed shaped the system's design
  • C. The Curse of Knowledge prevented Marcus from learning new time formats
  • D. A lack of self-directed learning caused Marcus to ignore user feedback
Correct answer: B. A mental model is a learned internal representation shaped by background and experience. Marcus's military upbringing created a mental model where 24-hour time is the default, and that model directly shaped his system design. Option A misapplies the Hype Cycle, which describes technology maturity stages. Option C confuses the Curse of Knowledge (difficulty imagining others' lack of knowledge) with mental models (the belief itself). Option D is about learning habits, not system design decisions.
Question 2
A senior developer at a software company is tasked with writing user documentation for a new inventory management tool. She writes instructions that skip several basic steps, assuming readers already know how to navigate databases. Junior employees report that the documentation is almost unusable for beginners.
Which phenomenon most directly explains why the documentation is difficult for beginners?
  • A. The developer's mental model of databases differs from the company standard
  • B. The 5-component IS model was not properly applied during system development
  • C. The Curse of Knowledge makes it hard for the developer to imagine not understanding databases
  • D. The software is in the Trough of Disillusionment phase of the Hype Cycle
Correct answer: C. The Curse of Knowledge describes how experts struggle to imagine what it's like not to know something, causing them to communicate poorly with beginners. The developer skipped steps she considers obvious. Option A involves mental models but doesn't explain the communication gap. Option B refers to IS components, not communication quality. Option D misapplies the Hype Cycle to an internal documentation issue.
Question 3
A venture capital firm invested $500 million in several AI startups in 2024. By mid-2025, survey data shows that large-firm AI adoption has actually declined from its peak of about 14%. The VC partners are surprised that their portfolio companies are struggling to find enterprise customers.
Which concept best explains the disconnect between the VC firm's investment and the market reality?
  • A. Anticipatory demand exceeded actual demand, reflecting the principle that investment does not equal adoption
  • B. The Curse of Knowledge prevented the VC partners from understanding AI technology
  • C. Network effects made it impossible for new AI startups to compete with incumbents
  • D. The 5-component IS model was incomplete because it lacked a people component
Correct answer: A. The principle that investment ≠ adoption ≠ diffusion explains this gap — speculative demand drove investment far beyond what actual enterprise usage supported. Option B misapplies the Curse of Knowledge, which is about communication, not investment decisions. Option C addresses network effects, which aren't the primary issue here. Option D is factually incorrect — the IS model does include people.
Question 4
A tech company launched a revolutionary wearable fitness tracker in 2022. Initial media coverage was extremely enthusiastic, calling it "the future of healthcare." By late 2023, several hospitals reported the device was inaccurate for clinical use, and investor interest dropped sharply. In 2025, the company quietly improved the sensor technology and began gaining traction with physical therapy clinics.
Which stage of the Gartner Hype Cycle does the company's 2025 resurgence best represent?
  • A. Peak of Inflated Expectations, because media coverage is generating renewed hype
  • B. Emerging Innovation, because the improved sensors represent a brand-new technology
  • C. Trough of Disillusionment, because investors have not yet returned
  • D. Slope of Enlightenment, because the product is finding practical value in specific use cases
Correct answer: D. The Slope of Enlightenment occurs when a technology matures and begins demonstrating real, practical benefits in targeted applications. The company improved its product and found a specific market (physical therapy clinics). Option A describes the earlier hype phase, not a quiet resurgence. Option B is wrong because the technology isn't new — it was improved. Option C describes the 2023 downturn, not the 2025 recovery.
Question 5
A small bakery owner decides to implement a new point-of-sale system. She purchases tablets (hardware) and downloads a POS app (software). The system stores transaction records (data) and her employees follow a defined checkout workflow (process). She also trains her staff to use the system (people).
According to the 5-component IS model, which component serves as the bridge between the computer side and the human side?
  • A. Software, because the POS app connects the hardware to the users
  • B. Data, because transaction records are shared between the technology and the people who use them
  • C. Process, because the checkout workflow links technology to business operations
  • D. Hardware, because the tablets physically sit between the software and the employees
Correct answer: B. In the 5-component IS model, Data serves as the bridge between the computer side (Hardware, Software) and the human side (Processes, People). Option A is incorrect because Software is an "instruction" on the computer side, not a bridge. Option C is wrong because Processes belong to the human side. Option D is wrong because Hardware is an "actor" on the computer side.
Chapter 2

Strategy & Technology

Key Vocabulary & Concepts

Competitive Advantage (CA)
The ability of a firm to consistently outperform its industry peers. To be meaningful, it must be sustainable — the firm must establish a difference it can preserve over time.
Source: Porter, M., Competitive Advantage (1985); Course slides, Ch 2.
Porter's Five Forces Model
An externally-focused framework describing five forces that determine the competitive intensity and profitability of an industry: (1) Rivalry among existing competitors, (2) Threat of New Entrants, (3) Threat of Substitute products/services, (4) Bargaining Power of Suppliers, and (5) Bargaining Power of Buyers.
Source: Porter, M., Competitive Strategy (1980); Course slides, Ch 2.
Examples — Strong rivalry: used car dealers. Weak rivalry: the IRS (no competitors). Strong new entrant threat: food trucks (low barriers). Weak: professional football teams (massive capital required).
Barriers to Entry
Obstacles that make it difficult for new firms to enter an industry and compete. High barriers to entry result in a low threat of new entrants. Types include: Capital Intensity (startup cost), Regulation (laws, intellectual property), Brand (established reputation), Distribution Channels (access to customers), Switching Costs, and Network Effects.
Source: Porter, M., Competitive Strategy (1980); Course slides, Ch 2.
Switching Costs (Lock-in)
The costs a consumer incurs when changing from one product or service to another. These can be monetary (contract termination fees), time and effort (learning a new system), or relational (re-creating social connections or data). High switching costs act as a barrier to entry for competitors.
Source: Gallaugher, J., Information Systems: A Manager's Guide to Harnessing Technology; Course slides, Ch 2.
Example: Moving from UT's email system to another provider would require importing/exporting years of messages and updating every account linked to that email.
Network Effects
A phenomenon where the value of a product or service increases as more people use it. Network effects create powerful barriers to entry because new competitors must overcome the value created by an existing user base.
Source: Shapiro, C. & Varian, H., Information Rules (1999); Course slides, Ch 2.
Example: Social media platforms are valuable because your friends are on them. eBay attracts sellers because it has the most buyers.
Porter's Model of Competitive Strategy
A 2×2 matrix classifying competitive strategies along two dimensions: competitive advantage source (Cost vs. Differentiation) and market scope (Broad vs. Narrow). This produces four strategies: (1) Broad Cost Leadership (e.g., Walmart), (2) Broad Differentiation (e.g., Amazon), (3) Narrow/Focused Cost (e.g., Dollar General, Aldi), and (4) Narrow/Focused Differentiation (e.g., Whole Foods, Rolex).
Source: Porter, M., Competitive Strategy (1980); Course slides, Ch 2.
Porter's Value Chain Model
The set of activities through which a product or service is created and delivered to customers. Primary Activities (revenue drivers that directly touch the product): Inbound Logistics, Operations, Outbound Logistics, Marketing & Sales, and Service. Support Activities (cost centers that don't directly touch the product): Infrastructure, HRM, Technology (R&D), and Procurement.
Source: Porter, M., Competitive Advantage (1985); Course slides, Ch 2.
Example: Apple Genius Bar employees are in the Service primary activity. Apple's R&D engineers who develop the next iPhone are in the Technology support activity.
Business Process
A defined sequence of steps or activities designed to produce a specific output or achieve a particular goal. Streamlining business processes is a key way IS enables competitive advantage.
Source: Course slides, Ch 2.
Example: An e-commerce order process: Log on → Place order → Save order → Pay → Assemble → Ship → Update customer record.
Strategy → IS Linkage
The five-step progression from strategy to information systems: (1) Examine Industry Structure, (2) Choose Competitive Strategy, (3) Link Value Chain Components, (4) Streamline Business Processes, (5) Design Information Systems.
Source: Course slides, Ch 2.

Chapter 2 Quiz

Question 1
Two college roommates want to open a restaurant near campus. They notice there are already 15 similar restaurants within a half-mile radius, all competing fiercely on price. Suppliers have many restaurant clients and can easily raise ingredient prices. Students can easily walk to a different spot for lunch.
According to Porter's Five Forces, which combination of forces most threatens the profitability of the roommates' planned restaurant?
  • A. Low rivalry and high bargaining power of suppliers
  • B. High threat of new entrants and low bargaining power of buyers
  • C. High rivalry, high bargaining power of suppliers, and high bargaining power of buyers
  • D. High threat of substitutes and low bargaining power of suppliers
Correct answer: C. The scenario describes 15 competing restaurants (high rivalry), suppliers who can raise prices (high supplier power), and students who can easily switch restaurants (high buyer power). Option A incorrectly states low rivalry. Option B incorrectly states low buyer power — students have many alternatives. Option D misidentifies the forces at play.
Question 2
Aldi operates with a narrow product selection, minimal store decor, and a self-service bagging model. Customers bring their own bags and return their own carts. This approach keeps Aldi's operating costs far below those of full-service grocery chains, allowing it to undercut competitors on price in specific geographic markets.
Which competitive strategy from Porter's model does Aldi's approach best represent?
  • A. Focused (narrow) cost leadership, targeting a specific market segment with the lowest prices
  • B. Broad differentiation, offering a unique shopping experience to the widest possible market
  • C. Broad cost leadership, competing on price across all grocery segments nationwide
  • D. Focused differentiation, offering premium organic products to health-conscious shoppers
Correct answer: A. Aldi targets a narrow market segment (budget-conscious consumers in specific areas) with a cost leadership approach (minimal services, low operating costs). Option B is wrong because Aldi doesn't differentiate — it competes on cost. Option C confuses narrow scope with broad scope. Option D misidentifies both the strategy (cost, not differentiation) and the product (budget staples, not premium organic).
Question 3
At a large electronics retailer, the IT team is responsible for managing the company's server infrastructure, overseeing network security, and maintaining internal software systems. These employees never interact directly with customers or physically handle any products being sold.
Where do these IT employees fit on Porter's Value Chain?
  • A. Operations, because they operate the company's technology systems
  • B. Service, because they provide technical support for the company
  • C. Inbound Logistics, because they manage the flow of data into the company
  • D. Infrastructure (support activity), because they handle general management systems that don't directly touch the product
Correct answer: D. IT infrastructure management falls under the Infrastructure support activity (which includes general management, planning, finance, and IS). Support activities are cost centers that don't directly touch the product. Option A confuses IT operations with the Operations primary activity (which transforms inputs into products). Option B confuses internal IT support with the Service primary activity (which provides after-sale support to customers). Option C misidentifies data management as a logistics function.
Question 4
A small startup sells handmade candles online. The founder is considering ways to make it harder for customers to leave for competitors. She's thinking about launching a loyalty program where customers accumulate points toward free candles, and building a subscription box that automatically delivers seasonal scents every quarter.
Which barrier to entry is the founder primarily trying to create?
  • A. Capital intensity, by requiring customers to make a large upfront investment
  • B. Switching costs, by making it costly in terms of lost rewards and convenience to leave
  • C. Network effects, by increasing the value of candles as more customers join
  • D. Regulation, by creating legal barriers that prevent competitors from copying her model
Correct answer: B. Loyalty points and auto-renewing subscriptions create switching costs — customers who leave would forfeit accumulated rewards and convenient delivery. Option A is incorrect because customers aren't making large upfront investments. Option C is wrong because buying candles doesn't become more valuable with more customers. Option D doesn't apply — there's no regulatory mechanism described.
Question 5
Southwest Airlines historically flew only Boeing 737s, served secondary airports in midsize cities, avoided hub-and-spoke routing, offered no assigned seating, and achieved 15-minute gate turnarounds — far faster than industry averages.
According to Michael Porter, what makes Southwest's approach a genuine strategy rather than just operational efficiency?
  • A. Southwest invested more money in technology than its competitors
  • B. Southwest copied the best practices of other low-cost carriers
  • C. Southwest deliberately chose a different, integrated set of activities to deliver a unique mix of value
  • D. Southwest achieved economies of scale by being the largest U.S. airline
Correct answer: C. Porter defines strategy as deliberately choosing a different set of activities to deliver a unique mix of value. Southwest's choices (single aircraft type, secondary airports, no frills, fast turnarounds) form an integrated, preservable system of differences. Option A confuses investment with strategy. Option B is the opposite of strategy — copying isn't differentiation. Option D confuses scale with strategic positioning.
Chapter 3

Platforms & Network Effects

Key Vocabulary & Concepts

Metcalfe's Law
The value of a network is proportional to the square of the number of its users (Value ≈ Users²). Named after Bob Metcalfe, inventor of Ethernet and UT Austin Professor of Innovation.
Source: Metcalfe, R. (1980); Course slides, Ch 3.
One-Sided Market
A market that derives most of its value from a single class of users. Features same-side exchange benefits — value comes from interaction among members of the same group.
Source: Gallaugher, J.; Course slides, Ch 3.
Example: A messaging app like WhatsApp — all users are the same type (message senders/receivers).
Two-Sided Market
A network market comprised of two distinct categories of participants. Both sides must deliver value for the network to function. Features cross-side exchange benefits — an increase in users on one side attracts more participants on the other side.
Source: Eisenmann, T., Parker, G., & Van Alstyne, M., "Strategies for Two-Sided Markets," Harvard Business Review (2006); Course slides, Ch 3.
Example: The video game industry — more gamers attract more game developers, and more games attract more gamers.
Platform
Products and services that allow for development and integration of software products and other complementary goods. "Products have features; platforms have communities."
Source: Course slides, Ch 3.
Examples: iOS App Store, Google Play, Windows, Roblox, Shopify.
Marginal Cost (of Software)
The cost of producing one additional unit of a product. For software, marginal cost is essentially zero — once the software is written, copying it costs virtually nothing. This leads to extremely profitable businesses for category-leading software products.
Source: Course slides, Ch 3.
Bundling
A pricing strategy where multiple products are sold together at a combined price lower than the sum of individual prices. Bundling captures consumer surplus by converting users who wouldn't buy each product individually into buyers of the bundle.
Source: Course slides, Ch 3.
Example: Microsoft Office bundled Word and Excel at $12 — both users bought the bundle, generating $24 in total revenue vs. only $20 if products were sold separately.
Konana's Software Ecosystem
A layered model of the software stack: Hardware → Operating System → Database Management System → Middleware → Enterprise Applications → Consumer Applications. Each layer depends on those below it, creating lock-in and switching costs at each level.
Source: Konana, P. (2007); Course slides, Ch 3.
Example: Web browsers are considered middleware because they sit between the OS and the applications that run on top of them.
Winner-Take-All Markets
Industries with strong network effects tend toward a single dominant player or standard, and at the extreme, a monopoly. The incumbent's advantage includes switching costs, complementary products, the existing user base, plus technological functionality — while a new entrant can only offer technological functionality.
Source: Shapiro, C. & Varian, H., Information Rules (1999); Course slides, Ch 3.
Strategies for Competing with Network Effects
Key approaches: (1) Move early, (2) Subsidize adoption, (3) Leverage viral promotion, (4) Use social proof, (5) Expand via convergence or envelopment, (6) Form alliances, (7) Encourage complementary goods development, (8) Maintain backward compatibility.
Source: Gallaugher, J.; Course slides, Ch 3.
Social Proof
The positive influence created when a person discovers that others are already doing something, making them more likely to do it too.
Source: Cialdini, R., Influence (1984); Course slides, Ch 3.
Convergence
When two or more markets, once considered distinctly separate, begin to offer the same features and capabilities.
Source: Course slides, Ch 3.
Example: The iPhone converged the phone, camera, music player, and web browser markets into one device.
Envelopment
When one market attempts to conquer a new market by making it a subset, component, or feature of its primary offering.
Source: Eisenmann, T., Parker, G., & Van Alstyne, M. (2011); Course slides, Ch 3.
Backward Compatibility
The ability of a new technology to take advantage of complementary products developed for a prior generation of technology, reducing switching costs for adopters.
Source: Course slides, Ch 3.
Example: Apple's Rosetta translation software allowed old PowerPC apps to run on new Intel-based Macs.
Freemium
A business model offering a free version of a product — sometimes with limited features or a time restriction — to allow customers to try it, with the goal of converting them into paying subscribers.
Source: Course slides, Ch 3.
Example: Zoom offered free video calls (limited to 40 minutes), then charged for unlimited meeting length.

Question 1
A college student creates a study group app where students can post notes and quiz each other. The app initially has 10 users on campus. Over the semester, it grows to 200 users. The original 10 users report that the app is dramatically more useful now because there are notes for nearly every course and it's easy to find study partners.
Which concept best explains why the app became more valuable as it grew?
  • A. Switching costs, because users invested time uploading their notes
  • B. Network effects, because the app's value increased as the number of users grew
  • C. Bundling, because the app combines notes and quizzes into one product
  • D. Backward compatibility, because older notes still work in the newer version of the app
Correct answer: B. Network effects occur when the value of a product increases as more people use it. With more users contributing notes and seeking study partners, the app becomes more useful for everyone. Option A describes lock-in, not value growth from user count. Option C misapplies bundling, which is a pricing strategy for combining products. Option D misidentifies backward compatibility, which relates to prior technology generations.
Question 2
A new food delivery startup builds an app that lets restaurants list their menus and lets customers order meals for delivery. The startup notices that when they sign up more restaurants, more customers download the app, and when more customers use the app, more restaurants want to join.
What type of market dynamic is this startup experiencing?
  • A. A one-sided market with same-side exchange benefits among restaurants
  • B. A winner-take-all market driven by high capital intensity
  • C. A freemium model where the free tier attracts paying customers
  • D. A two-sided market with cross-side exchange benefits between restaurants and customers
Correct answer: D. This is a two-sided market (two distinct participant types: restaurants and customers) exhibiting cross-side exchange benefits — growth on one side attracts participants on the other. Option A incorrectly labels it one-sided and misidentifies same-side benefits. Option B may eventually be true but doesn't describe the current dynamic. Option C describes a pricing model, not a market structure.
Question 3
A tech startup has built a social media platform that is technically superior to Instagram in every way — better video quality, faster loading, better privacy controls, and no ads. Despite heavy marketing, the startup struggles to attract users because everyone's friends, followers, and content libraries are already on Instagram.
Which concept best explains why technological superiority alone is not enough for this startup to succeed?
  • A. In network markets, an incumbent's advantages from switching costs, complementary products, and user base outweigh a new entrant's technical functionality alone
  • B. The startup failed to implement a freemium model to attract initial users
  • C. The startup's competitive strategy was too broad in scope to compete effectively
  • D. The startup didn't maintain backward compatibility with Instagram's older features
Correct answer: A. In network effect markets, incumbent products benefit from switching costs, complementary products (content, third-party integrations), and the existing user base — all of which a new entrant with only technological functionality cannot match. Option B may be a valid tactic but doesn't explain the core structural problem. Option C misapplies Porter's competitive strategy framework. Option D misuses backward compatibility, which refers to prior technology generations, not competitor compatibility.
Question 4
In the early 2000s, the smartphone market was fragmented — dozens of phone manufacturers each ran different operating systems with different screen sizes and capabilities. App developers struggled to write software that worked on more than a few devices. Then Apple launched the iPhone with iOS and a unified App Store.
How did the iPhone's approach help solve the app development problem?
  • A. By using a freemium pricing model that attracted more developers
  • B. By forming alliances with all existing phone manufacturers
  • C. By creating a platform with a standardized ecosystem, making it worthwhile for developers to invest in building apps
  • D. By offering backward compatibility with apps written for older Nokia phones
Correct answer: C. Apple created a platform (iOS + App Store) that standardized the hardware and software environment, so developers could write one app and reach millions of users. This is also an example of convergence — the iPhone merged the phone, camera, music player, and web browser markets. Option A describes a pricing model, not a platform strategy. Option B is factually incorrect. Option D is incorrect — the iPhone intentionally did not support older phone apps.
Question 5
When Zoom launched, it offered a free tier that allowed anyone to host 40-minute video meetings with no account setup complexity. Users could invite others by simply sharing a link, and those invitees often signed up for their own accounts. During the 2020 pandemic, Zoom also gave free unlimited access to all K-12 schools.
Which combination of strategies for competing in network effect markets did Zoom employ?
  • A. Envelopment and backward compatibility
  • B. Freemium model, viral promotion, and subsidized adoption
  • C. Capital intensity and raising barriers to entry through regulation
  • D. Convergence and forming alliances with competitors like WebEx
Correct answer: B. Zoom used a freemium model (free 40-minute tier), viral promotion (easy link sharing that recruited new users), and subsidized adoption (free service to K-12 schools). Option A doesn't apply — Zoom didn't envelope another market or rely on backward compatibility. Option C misapplies barriers to entry concepts. Option D is incorrect — Zoom competed against WebEx, not allied with it, and the scenario doesn't describe convergence.
Chapter 4

Disruptive Technologies

Key Vocabulary & Concepts

Disruptive Innovation
An innovation that initially enters the market with attributes existing customers don't value, often targeting low-end or new consumer segments. Over time, the disruptive product improves until it invades and eventually overtakes established markets. Contrasts with sustaining innovation, which makes incremental improvements to existing products.
Source: Christensen, C., The Innovator's Dilemma (1997); Course slides, Ch 4.
Example: The original iPhone (2007) was a disruptive innovation. Each subsequent iPhone upgrade (iPhone 15 → 16) is a sustaining innovation.
Sustaining Innovation
Incremental improvements to existing products that serve current customers better. Does not create new markets or value networks.
Source: Christensen, C., The Innovator's Dilemma (1997); Course slides, Ch 4.
Enabling Technology
A technology that makes products or services affordable and accessible to wider populations who previously could not use them.
Source: Course slides, Ch 4.
The Long Tail
A business model concept where digital businesses can profit from selling a large number of niche products in small quantities, rather than relying solely on a few bestsellers. Physical retailers cannot stock such items because of shelf space constraints, but digital platforms have virtually unlimited inventory capacity.
Source: Anderson, C., The Long Tail (2006); Course slides, Ch 4.
Example: Netflix and Amazon can offer obscure titles that a physical store like Blockbuster could never justify stocking.
Why Big Firms Fail at Disruption
Established firms often fail to respond to disruptive innovation because: (1) they don't see disruptions as threats, (2) they lack resources allocated to unproven technologies, and (3) they ignore the needs of new, unproven customer segments. Solutions: talk to researchers and VCs, rotate staff, watch for employee departures to startups, and invest in startups.
Source: Christensen, C., The Innovator's Dilemma (1997); Course slides, Ch 4.
Example: Blockbuster turned down buying Netflix for $50 million in 2000.
Cloud Computing (as disruptive technology)
Cloud computing was the foundational disruptive technology that threatened traditional desktop software companies like Intuit. Intuit survived by acquiring Mint, migrating to cloud, and using AI — connected services now represent over 65% of revenue.
Source: Course slides, Ch 4.

Chapter 4 Quiz

Question 1
In 2000, Blockbuster was the dominant video rental chain with thousands of stores nationwide. Netflix approached Blockbuster with an offer to be acquired for $50 million. Blockbuster's executives laughed off the offer, viewing Netflix's mail-order DVD service as a niche product that couldn't compete with their extensive store network and brand recognition.
Which characteristic of disruptive innovation best explains why Blockbuster failed to recognize Netflix as a threat?
  • A. Netflix offered a sustaining innovation that directly improved upon Blockbuster's existing services
  • B. Netflix had stronger network effects than Blockbuster's store locations
  • C. Netflix's high capital intensity made it impossible for Blockbuster to compete
  • D. Disruptive innovations initially target underserved segments with attributes incumbents don't value, making them easy to dismiss
Correct answer: D. Disruptive innovations enter markets with attributes existing customers don't initially value — Netflix's mail-order model seemed inferior to in-store browsing. Blockbuster dismissed it because it didn't serve their core customers. Option A is wrong because Netflix was disruptive, not sustaining. Option B misapplies network effects. Option C is incorrect — Netflix actually had lower capital requirements than Blockbuster's physical stores.
Question 2
A student runs a small Etsy shop selling vintage concert T-shirts. She stocks about 300 unique designs. Meanwhile, Amazon and eBay list millions of vintage T-shirts, including extremely obscure bands that would never justify shelf space in a physical retail store.
Which concept best explains why platforms like Amazon and eBay can profitably offer such an enormous selection of niche items?
  • A. The Long Tail — digital platforms profit from selling many niche products in small quantities because they don't face physical shelf space constraints
  • B. Sustaining innovation — Amazon continuously improves its existing product selection
  • C. Bundling — Amazon packages niche T-shirts with popular products to increase overall sales
  • D. Backward compatibility — Amazon supports older T-shirt designs from prior decades
Correct answer: A. The Long Tail explains how digital platforms can profitably stock huge inventories of niche products because they aren't limited by physical shelf space. Option B misapplies sustaining innovation, which is about product improvements. Option C misuses bundling — there's no product packaging strategy described. Option D humorously misapplies backward compatibility.
Question 3
Apple releases the iPhone 16 with a slightly better camera, a faster processor, and improved battery life compared to the iPhone 15. The core design, operating system, and target market remain essentially the same.
What type of innovation does the iPhone 16 represent?
  • A. Disruptive innovation, because it introduces new technology that could replace older phones
  • B. Enabling technology, because it makes smartphones affordable to new populations
  • C. Sustaining innovation, because it makes incremental improvements to serve existing customers better
  • D. Convergence, because it combines multiple technologies into a single device
Correct answer: C. Sustaining innovation involves incremental improvements to existing products for current customers — better camera, faster processor, and longer battery in the same product category. Option A confuses any new technology with disruption — disruption requires targeting new or underserved markets. Option B is wrong because the iPhone 16 isn't making smartphones newly affordable. Option D was true of the original iPhone but not an incremental upgrade.

Zara & Supply Chain Management

Key Vocabulary & Concepts

Vertical Integration
When a firm owns or controls multiple layers of its supply chain — from design through manufacturing to retail distribution. This gives the firm more control over quality, speed, and costs, but requires significant capital investment.
Source: Course slides, Ch 5; Porter, M., Competitive Strategy (1980).
Example: Zara designs, manufactures, and distributes its own clothing through company-owned stores, while Gap outsources manufacturing to third-party factories.
Zara's Fast Fashion Model
Zara can move a design from concept to store shelf in approximately 15 days (rivals take a full season). Key elements: in-house designers creating knock-offs of trending styles, just-in-time manufacturing, RFID tags for inventory tracking, and vertical integration. Zara spends less on technology than the industry average but uses it strategically. Higher manufacturing costs are offset by fewer markdowns, faster inventory turnover, less advertising, and reduced risk. Guiding principle: "Inventory = Death."
Source: Gallaugher, J.; Course slides, Ch 5.
Supply Chain Management (SCM)
The coordination of relationships and activities across firms involved in creating and delivering a product or service to the end customer, with the goal of maximizing value and efficiency across the entire chain.
Source: Course slides, Ch 5.
Disintermediation
The removal of intermediaries (middlemen) from a supply chain, allowing producers to sell directly to consumers. Reduces costs but also removes services that intermediaries provided.
Source: Course slides, Ch 5.
Example: Dell sells computers directly to customers, bypassing retail stores. Zara's vertical integration means it sells directly through its own stores.
Bullwhip Effect
A phenomenon where small fluctuations in consumer demand become amplified as orders move up the supply chain, resulting in increasingly large swings in inventory and ordering at each stage. It is NOT caused by erratic consumer demand — it is caused by the multistage nature of supply chains and is worsened by longer lead times. The fix is sharing actual consumer demand data across all supply chain participants.
Source: Lee, H., Padmanabhan, V., & Whang, S., "The Bullwhip Effect in Supply Chains," Sloan Management Review (1997); Course slides, Ch 5.
Contract Manufacturing
Outsourcing production to third-party manufacturers, typically in lower-cost countries. Offers lower costs but carries risks including sweatshop labor, brand damage, and reduced quality control. Service Level Agreements (SLAs) attempt to shift responsibility to third-party manufacturers.
Source: Course slides, Ch 5.
Fair Factories Clearinghouse
An initiative created by Reebok that allows firms to share audit information on overseas manufacturers, improving transparency and accountability in global supply chains.
Source: Course slides, Ch 5.
RFID (Radio-Frequency Identification)
A technology using electromagnetic fields to identify and track tags attached to objects. Used by Zara to track inventory in real time throughout its supply chain.
Source: Course slides, Ch 5.

Moore's Law & Hardware

Key Vocabulary & Concepts

Moore's Law
The observation that the number of transistors on a microchip doubles approximately every 18 months, with corresponding decreases in cost per transistor. It is not a law of physics but an industry observation by Intel co-founder Gordon Moore that became a self-fulfilling industry business plan. AI capabilities are currently doubling approximately every 6 months.
Source: Moore, G., "Cramming More Components onto Integrated Circuits," Electronics (1965); Course slides, Ch 6.
Price Elasticity of Technology Demand
The demand for technology is highly elastic — as computing becomes cheaper (per Moore's Law), demand increases significantly and entirely new applications become feasible.
Source: Course slides, Ch 6.
Example: Cheap processing power made smartphones, streaming video, and cloud services possible — uses that didn't exist when computing was expensive.
Semiconductor / Transistor / Chip
A semiconductor is a material that conducts electricity conditionally. A transistor is a tiny binary switch (representing 1s and 0s) built from semiconductor material. A chip (integrated circuit) contains billions of transistors on a single piece of silicon.
Source: Course slides, Ch 6.
Bits, Bytes, Bandwidth, and Latency
8 bits = 1 byte. Storage is measured in Bytes (capital B); transfer speeds in bits per second (lowercase b). Divide Mbps by 8 to get MB/s. Bandwidth = the volume of data that can travel simultaneously (like highway lanes). Latency = the time for data to travel from source to destination (like the speed limit). Streaming needs high bandwidth; gaming needs low latency.
Source: Course slides, Ch 6.
GPU (Graphics Processing Unit) / ASIC
Originally designed for rendering graphics, GPUs are now ideal for AI workloads because they can perform many calculations simultaneously (parallel processing). GPUs are a type of ASIC (Application-Specific Integrated Circuit) — a chip designed for a particular use rather than general-purpose computing. NVIDIA dominates the high-end AI chip market (80–95% market share).
Source: Course slides, Ch 6.
Multicore Processors
CPUs with multiple independent processing cores on a single chip. They run cooler and use less power than equivalent single-core processors, enabling improved performance through parallel task execution.
Source: Course slides, Ch 6.
Fab (Fabrication Facility)
A semiconductor manufacturing plant that costs $20 billion or more to build and requires massive amounts of power and water. Most high-end chips are made by TSMC (Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company).
Source: Course slides, Ch 6.
CHIPS and Science Act (2022)
U.S. legislation providing $52 billion in subsidies for domestic semiconductor production, $24 billion in tax credits, and over $170 billion for research, aimed at reducing dependence on foreign chip manufacturing (particularly TSMC in Taiwan).
Source: CHIPS and Science Act, Pub. L. 117-167 (2022); Course slides, Ch 6.
Compiler
A program that converts human-readable developer code into machine-executable instructions that a processor can run. Different processors may require different compilers.
Source: Course slides, Ch 6.
Apple M-Series Chips & Rosetta 2
Apple's custom ARM-based processors (M1, M2, etc.) are designed by Apple and manufactured by TSMC. They use optimized instruction pathways for better performance and efficiency. Rosetta 2 is an emulator that maintains backward compatibility by allowing software written for older Intel-based Macs to run on the new M-series chips.
Source: Course slides, Ch 6.
E-Waste
Discarded electronic devices. Globally, 62 million tons were generated in 2022, projected to reach 74 million tons by 2030. Contains toxic substances (lead, cadmium, mercury). There is no comprehensive U.S. federal recycling law. Apple's Daisy robot can recycle 200 iPhones per hour.
Source: Global E-waste Monitor (2024); Course slides, Ch 6.
Disney MagicBand / RFID Wearable
Disney's RFID-enabled wristband used for park admission, hotel room keys, payments, and location tracking. Enables big data analytics that reduced turnstile wait times by 30% and allowed Disney to serve 3,000–5,000 additional guests daily.
Source: Course slides, Ch 6.

The complete cost of acquiring and operating a technology system over its lifetime. Goes far beyond the purchase price to include requirements analysis, implementation, training, ongoing support, and strategic development. The purchase price is roughly 20% of total costs; hidden costs account for approximately 80%.

Open Source Software

Key Vocabulary & Concepts

Open Source Software (OSS)
Software whose source code is freely available for anyone to view, modify, and distribute. OSS vendors generate revenue not from selling code but from support, consulting, security compliance, hosting, and premium enterprise tools.
Source: Course slides, Ch 8; Open Source Initiative, opensource.org.
Closed Source (Proprietary) Software
Software where the source code is treated as intellectual property and is not publicly available. Revenue comes from licensing fees, subscriptions, or download charges.
Source: Course slides, Ch 8.
Linux
An open source operating system kernel created in 1991 by Linus Torvalds. Linux is the backbone of global internet infrastructure — even Microsoft Azure runs on it. The Linux Foundation (formed 2007) was created when rival tech companies (IBM, HP, Intel, Oracle) realized shared neutral infrastructure was cheaper than building proprietary operating systems.
Source: Torvalds, L. & Diamond, D., Just for Fun (2001); Course slides, Ch 8.
Free Software Foundation (FSF)
Founded by Richard Stallman in 1983 to promote the idea that software source code should be freely shared. Stallman's philosophy started the free software movement; Linus Torvalds later provided the practical kernel (Linux) that brought the vision to widespread use.
Source: Course slides, Ch 8.
Bus Factor
The risk created when critical open source infrastructure is maintained by a small number of unpaid volunteers. If those volunteers stop contributing (figuratively, "get hit by a bus"), the entire project could collapse. The Linux Foundation was partly formed to address this problem.
Source: Course slides, Ch 8.
Tech Stacks
Coordinated layers of software used together to build and run applications. LAMP (Linux, Apache, MySQL, PHP/Python) is the classic open source stack powering 40%+ of websites. MEAN (MongoDB, Express.js, Angular, Node.js) is the modern all-JavaScript stack for real-time applications.
Source: Course slides, Ch 8.
LAMP examples: Facebook, YouTube, Wikipedia, Slack. MEAN examples: Netflix, Uber.
Compiled vs. Interpreted Languages
Compiled languages (C++, C#) are converted entirely into machine code before execution, making them faster. Interpreted/scripting languages (Python, JavaScript, Java) are translated line-by-line at runtime, making them easier to write and test but generally slower.
Source: Course slides, Ch 8.
OSS Advantages
Free to use, more reliable ("many eyes" find bugs), more secure (code is publicly auditable), scalable, and faster time to market. Consumer OSS apps are less popular because infrastructure-level projects get more community support, consumers value source code access less, and TCO can be higher without a support contract.
Source: Course slides, Ch 8.

Which OSS business model does Red Hat's approach represent?
  • A. Freemium, because Red Hat offers a free version with limited features

Which type of programming language is most appropriate for this task, and why?
  • A. A compiled language like C++, because compiled code is always better for data analysis
  • B. Assembly language, because it gives the most direct control over the hardware

Option A overvalues execution speed when the scenario says it's not a priority. Option B is impractical for data analysis. Option D incorrectly states that all data analysis requires a DBMS.
Chapter 9

Software as a Service (SaaS)

Key Vocabulary & Concepts

Cloud Computing
The outsourcing of technology stack management to a third-party provider. Instead of owning and maintaining hardware, software, and databases on-site, firms rent these resources from cloud providers who manage them remotely.
Source: Course slides, Ch 9; NIST SP 800-145.
IaaS (Infrastructure as a Service)
A cloud model where the provider manages the hardware (servers, storage, networking) and the customer manages everything above it (OS, middleware, applications, data). The customer rents raw computing infrastructure.
Source: Course slides, Ch 9.
Examples: Netflix uses AWS; Walmart uses Azure.
PaaS (Platform as a Service)
A cloud model where the provider manages hardware, OS, middleware, and development tools. The customer only builds and manages their own applications on top of the platform.
Source: Course slides, Ch 9.
Example: Salesforce lets customers build custom applications on its platform.
SaaS (Software as a Service)
A cloud model where the provider manages the entire stack — hardware, OS, DBMS, middleware, and the application itself. The customer simply uses the software through a web browser or app. Business models include subscriptions, freemium, and ad-supported.
Source: Course slides, Ch 9.
Examples: Canvas, Office 365, Google Sheets, Salesforce CRM.
SaaS Benefits
Faster deployment, lower upfront costs (no hardware/software/DBMS purchase), better hardware quality from specialized providers, remote access from anywhere, elastic scalability, and more predictable costs. Developers benefit from maintaining a single platform, easy deployment of updates, and no piracy problem (code never leaves the server).
Source: Course slides, Ch 9.
SaaS Risks
Vendor dependency (lock-in), forced migration/switching costs if the vendor changes terms, latency issues, missing features compared to desktop versions, data stored off-site raising security/legal concerns, and WiFi dependency. However, major cloud providers (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud) generally have better security than most individual enterprises.
Source: Course slides, Ch 9.
Why Firms Still Use On-Premise Software
Unique business processes that can't be standardized for cloud delivery, perceived security threats (even though major cloud providers are often more secure), and regulatory constraints that require data to remain on-site.
Source: Course slides, Ch 9.
TCO: SaaS vs. Traditional Software
Over time, SaaS typically has much lower and more predictable costs than traditional licensed software. Traditional software costs spike at implementation, integration, and upgrade cycles, whereas SaaS costs remain relatively flat.
Source: Course slides, Ch 9.

Chapter 10

Software Project Management

Key Vocabulary & Concepts

Systems Development
The process of creating and maintaining information systems. Involves all 5 IS components (Hardware, Software, Data, Processes, People) — not just programming. Requires business knowledge and management skill alongside technical expertise. There are no pure "IT projects" — they're business projects with an IT component.
Source: Course slides, Ch 10.
Brooks's Law
Adding more people to a late software project makes it later. New team members need time to learn the project and increase communication overhead, slowing the team down. Named after Fred Brooks, author of The Mythical Man-Month.
Source: Brooks, F., The Mythical Man-Month (1975); Course slides, Ch 10.
Project Management Triple Constraint
Three competing constraints on every project: Scope (what gets built), Resources (money, people, equipment), and Schedule (time). You cannot optimize all three simultaneously — improving one typically requires sacrificing another.
Source: Course slides, Ch 10; PMI, PMBOK Guide.
Waterfall Methodology
A linear, sequential development approach where all requirements are gathered upfront, and each phase (requirements → design → development → testing → deployment) must be completed before the next begins. Works for civil engineering but causes many software project failures because requirements often change mid-project.
Source: Course slides, Ch 10.
Agile Methodology
An iterative, continuous development approach with frequent rollouts. The dominant modern methodology. Development cycles through: Plan → Design → Develop → Test → Deploy → Review, with each iteration producing a working increment. Techniques include test-driven design, pair programming, extreme programming, and refactoring.
Source: Beck, K. et al., Manifesto for Agile Software Development (2001); Course slides, Ch 10.
Prototypes & Wireframes
Mock-ups used to help users evaluate requirements, test interfaces, and communicate with developers. A risk is that prototypes can make things look more complete than they actually are, creating unrealistic expectations.
Source: Course slides, Ch 10.
Low Code / No Code (LCNC)
Visual development tools that allow "citizen developers" (non-programmers) to build applications with minimal coding. The market was approximately $27 billion in 2023. Concerns include inconsistent data, duplicate costs, regulatory violations (HIPAA, FERPA, GDPR), security issues, and platform dependency.
Source: Course slides, Ch 10.
Examples: Google AppSheet, Microsoft Power Apps, Oracle APEX, Salesforce Lightning.
IDE (Integrated Development Environment)
A software tool that bundles a code editor, debugger, and compiler into one application, providing a complete environment for writing, testing, and compiling code.
Source: Course slides, Ch 10.
Why Tech Projects Fail
Common causes: poor goals, weak leadership, lack of executive commitment, bad resource forecasting, scope creep (uncontrolled expansion of project requirements), poor communication, immature technology choices, inadequate testing, organizational politics, and time pressure.
Source: Course slides, Ch 10.
Scope Creep
The uncontrolled expansion of a project's requirements beyond the original plan, often caused by stakeholders adding features without adjusting the schedule or budget. Business users must take responsibility for managing scope creep.
Source: Course slides, Ch 10.
HealthCare.gov Case Study
Launched October 2013 with critical bugs — couldn't handle demand, had no performance dashboard, and terrible UX. Rescued by bringing in clear leadership and experienced technologists who used Agile practices (stand-up meetings, clear priorities). Fixed within approximately 2 months; over 8 million users signed up. A case study in how Agile rescued a Waterfall failure.
Source: Course slides, Ch 10.
User Responsibility for Requirements
As a future business manager, taking responsibility for system requirements is your most important job. You must ensure complete and accurate requirements, manage scope creep, define test conditions, and actively participate throughout development. "Give IS pros good directions."
Source: Course slides, Ch 10.

A software team is three weeks behind schedule on a major release. The project manager's boss suggests hiring five additional developers immediately to get back on track. The lead developer warns that bringing new people in at this stage will actually make things worse.
